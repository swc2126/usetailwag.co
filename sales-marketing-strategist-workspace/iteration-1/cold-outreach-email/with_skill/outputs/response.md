# Cold Outreach Email for Daycare Owners

## The Strategy

Cold email to daycare owners you find on Google is a solid play — you're reaching people actively running a business, and email gets past the gatekeeper (usually just the daycare's general inbox). But generic "check out our platform" emails bounce off. What works here is **specificity and proof**, not pitch.

Your email needs to:
1. **Show you did research** — mention something specific about their daycare (their Google rating, review count, or a detail from their photos/posts)
2. **Lead with their problem, not your solution** — acknowledge the gap between happy clients and actual reviews
3. **Make it about them, not you** — don't lead with TailWag; lead with the result
4. **Lower the friction to say yes** — offer a quick call or a free 10-minute demo, not a sales presentation

The goal isn't to close in the email — it's to get a "let me see how this works" conversation. That conversation is where you learn what matters to them and they see the magic.

## The Email

**Subject line options** (pick one or test both):
- `Quick question about your Google reviews — [Daycare Name]`
- `12 new 5-star reviews in 30 days — worth 5 min?`
- `[Daycare Name] — we might be able to add $400-500/month to your business`

---

**Email body:**

Hi [Owner Name],

I just spent 10 minutes looking through [Daycare Name]'s Google reviews and Instagram, and two things jumped out at me:

1. You have tons of happy clients (your last review said "Best daycare in [area]!")
2. But you're probably only capturing reviews from 1 in 20 of them

That gap is costing you. Clients shop by reviews. Most daycares miss 60–70% of 5-star reviews they could be getting.

I'm building something specifically for independent daycares to fix this — it's an SMS platform that sends a personalized text to each client at pickup (a quick report card about their dog's day), and at the right moment, asks them to leave a review if they loved it. Setup is 5 minutes, and it runs in the background.

Here's what I'm seeing with early users: one daycare got 12 new reviews in the first month. Another recovered 3 no-shows in week one (that's $180/week right there).

I'm not going to pitch you on a call — but if you're open to it, I'd love to show you exactly how it would work for [Daycare Name] in 10 minutes. No obligation, just a quick look.

Does Wednesday or Thursday work for a quick call?

## How to Use This

### Before You Send
1. **Personalize the research section** — don't just mention reviews; pick one or two specific things you actually noticed. If they have 47 reviews and a 4.8 rating, say that. If their Instagram shows a fun team or a cute dog story, reference it. People smell generic from a mile away.

2. **Find the right email address** — Google the daycare name + "email" or check their website footer. Failing that, try the format `firstname@daycarename.com` or `owner@daycarename.com`. If it bounces, you have a reason to call or visit instead.

3. **Adjust the dollar numbers if needed** — $400–500/month is based on typical no-show recovery + review-driven bookings for a small daycare. If you've talked to daycare owners and learned their numbers are different, change it. Specificity beats round numbers.

### Success Metrics
- **Open rate**: 20–30% is decent for cold email. Personalization helps.
- **Reply rate**: Aim for 5–10%. If you're getting 0/10, the subject line or first sentence isn't working — try a different angle.
- **Meeting rate**: Of replies, 50%+ should say yes to a quick call. If not, you're being too sales-y or the email is too long.

### Follow-Up Sequence (if no reply)
- **Day 3**: Short follow-up — "Hey, wanted to make sure this landed. Would Wed/Thu still work?"
- **Day 7**: Add value — share a relevant stat or article about review generation for daycares
- **Day 10**: Soft close — "I'm only reaching out to 20 daycares in DFW this quarter. This is my last nudge — let me know if you want to see the demo."
- **After day 10**: Stop. Move to the next prospect.

### Alternative: Phone Call Opener
If you find a phone number, calling first can work better than email — especially if you have a hook: "I was just looking at your Google reviews and noticed [specific thing], thought you might want to see something in 10 minutes." But email scales better if you have 50 daycares to reach.
