# TailWag Go-to-Market Plan: Getting Your First 10 Customers

## The Strategy

Your first 10 customers aren't a sales problem — they're a research problem. You're not scaling yet. You're proving that daycare owners actually want what you're building, understanding what makes them say yes, and getting testimonials you can use to get customer 11.

That means every conversation you have before launch is *gold*. You're not trying to hit a number; you're trying to have five to ten conversations with daycare owners who get what TailWag solves and are willing to try it.

Here's the philosophy:
- **Go hyperlocal.** Irving/DFW is your backyard. Use that. Visit daycares in person. Call the ones that look promising. You'll learn more in 30 minutes of face time than in 30 emails.
- **Start with warm.** You're pre-launch. Every first conversation should be because someone you know referred you, or because you did homework on their specific situation. Cold is a last resort.
- **Lead with value, not features.** Don't pitch TailWag yet. Ask questions. Find the pain. *Then* show how TailWag solves it.
- **Make it easy to say yes.** Offer a free trial, a setup on your dime, a money-back guarantee. Remove every reason to say no.

The goal: Get 3-4 early adopters running TailWag by the end of April. They become your proof points for customer 5-10.

## The Action Plan

### Phase 1: Build Your Prospect List & Do Homework (Week 1)

**Step 1: List all daycares in Irving and the immediate DFW area.**
- Use Google Maps. Search "dog daycare Irving TX," "doggie daycare Dallas," "pet boarding near me."
- Create a spreadsheet with: business name, address, phone, Google rating, number of reviews, website URL, Instagram handle (if they have one).
- Aim for 40-50 prospects. You're looking for:
  - Daycares with **low review counts** (under 20 reviews). These owners are hungry for ways to get more visibility.
  - Daycares with **4.5-star ratings** (not 5 stars). They have room to grow and room to improve.
  - Daycares that post on **Instagram regularly**. They're social-savvy enough to get TailWag's value, visual enough to love the report cards.
  - **Independently owned** daycares (not franchises — they're slower to buy). Look for single-location operations.

**Step 2: Research each prospect.**
- Spend 5 minutes per daycare. Read their Google reviews. Do they mention slow communication? Dogs not hearing about their day? Reviews that aren't getting traction? These are your pain points.
- Check their website. Are they basic? Are they updated? This tells you their tech comfort level.
- Look at their Instagram. Do they post daily? Weekly? This tells you how engaged they are with marketing.
- Note: Any mention of problems like "wish I heard more updates," "communication could be better," or "hard to know what happened to my dog" = hot prospect. Highlight these.

**Deliverable:** A spreadsheet named `irving-daycare-prospects.csv` with columns for Name, Phone, Google Rating, Review Count, Pain Signals, Warm Intro Opportunity (do you know anyone who knows them?), and Priority (1-3).

---

### Phase 2: Warm Intros & Initial Outreach (Week 1-2)

**Step 3: Find warm introduction paths.**
- Do you know anyone who owns a daycare, has a dog in daycare, or knows a daycare owner? Start there. A text like *"Hey, I'm building something for dog daycares. Do you know anyone at [Daycare Name] I could talk to?"* opens doors.
- Check your personal network: veterinarians, dog trainers, groomers, pet supply store owners. They're all adjacent to your market and often know daycare owners.
- Join Facebook groups: Search for "doggie daycare owners DFW" and "pet business owners Texas." Spend one week just reading and learning. When you see someone complaining about communication or reviews, that's a prospect AND a teaching moment.
- Cold option: If there's no warm intro, that's fine. You'll call directly (see Step 4).

**Step 4: Make your first calls.**
- Script (keep it conversational):
  > *Hi [Owner Name], this is Summer [Last Name] calling from a company called TailWag. I'm reaching out to a few daycares in the area because I'm building something I think could help you. Do you have five minutes?*
  >
  > [If yes:] *Great. I noticed on Google that you have [X reviews/low review count/great ratings] and I know from working with other daycares that getting more updates and reviews from clients is a constant challenge. I'm testing a tool that automates personalized report cards and review requests. It's free for the first month, and it takes about 5 minutes to set up. Would you be open to a quick conversation about how it works?*

- Don't pitch. Ask questions:
  - *"How do you currently tell pet parents about their dog's day?"*
  - *"How many Google reviews would you like to get this quarter?"*
  - *"What's the biggest pain point with managing client communication?"*
  - *"If you could automate one thing about your day, what would it be?"*

- Success = you get a *"Yeah, I'm curious"* or a *"Let's set up a time to talk more."* You're not looking for a yes to buy yet. You're looking for curiosity.

**Deliverable:** A call log with notes on each conversation. Track: what they said about their pain, whether they're a fit, whether they want a demo, next steps.

---

### Phase 3: Demo & Trial Setup (Week 2-3)

**Step 5: Prep a killer demo.**
- Use their actual daycare name and real dogs from their Instagram if possible.
- Show three things:
  1. **The report card.** Make it personal. Reference something specific to their business (e.g., a dog they posted about, a seasonal thing like spring zoomies).
  2. **The impact.** Show how a message from TailWag gets 40+ characters of personality and care in their customer's hands, every single day.
  3. **The result.** "This is what it looks like when you get five more reviews in a month. Here's the ROI: each review gets you one call, each call gets you $100-150 in recurring revenue."

- Demo format: You can do this over Zoom, or in person at their daycare (even better — you get to see their operation and they feel the personal touch).

**Step 6: Offer a trial.**
- Frame: *"I want to set this up for free. You don't pay anything for the first month. At the end of the month, if you're getting value — more reviews, happier clients, fewer no-shows — let's talk about making it permanent. If you're not seeing value, no hard feelings."*
- Make setup stupid easy. You set it up for them. They just give you:
  - Their business name (you probably already have it)
  - Opening/closing times
  - Dog names (you can pull these from Instagram)
  - Client phone numbers (they have this in their booking system)

- Don't make them do work. You do the work. That's the sell.

**Step 7: First report card goes out same day.**
- This is critical. Show them real-time. The first text goes out to a real client that evening or the next morning.
- Have them show you when a client responds. That's the moment they *get it*. The client gets a smile, the daycare owner gets proof, and TailWag is already working.

**Deliverable:** A demo screen recording or deck they can reference. A trial onboarding checklist so you don't miss anything.

---

### Phase 4: Follow-Up Sequence (Week 3-4)

**Step 8: Build a follow-up sequence for trials.**

- **Day 1:** Send a thank-you. *"Thanks for being willing to try TailWag. Your first report cards go out tonight. I'll check in with you in a few days to see how clients are responding."*
- **Day 3-4:** Quick check-in. *"How are things going? Any feedback from clients on the report cards yet?"* Listen. Adjust if needed.
- **Day 7:** Value add. Share something useful. An article about review generation in the pet industry, or a stat like *"Daycares that send daily updates see 40% fewer no-shows."* Keep it brief.
- **Day 10:** Gentle close. *"You're a week in. Have the report cards been hitting the mark? What would it take for you to keep TailWag running after this month?"* Get their objection. Fix it.
- **Day 14:** Final ask. *"I'd love to have you as one of our first customers. Here's what I'm thinking: [pricing], and I'll handle your setup completely. Let's chat about making this real."*

**Step 9: Track results weekly.**
- Pull their metrics: How many texts went out? How many got responses? How many reviews came in? How many no-shows decreased?
- Use these numbers in your close and in your next pitch. *"Hey, I wanted to show you — in two weeks, your clients responded to 34 personalized report cards and you got 4 new reviews. That's worth $400-600 in new revenue just from increased bookings."*

**Deliverable:** A CRM or simple spreadsheet tracking: prospect name, trial start date, texts sent, responses, reviews generated, whether they've converted to paid, next action.

---

### Phase 5: Scale to 10 (Week 4+)

**Step 10: Use your early wins to get numbers 6-10.**
- By end of April, you should have 3-4 trials running. At least one should be seeing real results.
- Use that customer's permission to reference them with prospects 6-10. *"Hey, I'm talking to [Daycare Name] right now and they've gotten 5 new reviews in three weeks. Would you want to see how this is working for them?"*
- Ask for testimonials from your early customers. A 20-second video of them saying *"TailWag is saving me time and getting my clients talking about us"* is worth more than your best pitch.
- Repeat the process: warm intro → call → demo → trial → follow-up → close.

**The asks get easier.** By customer 6, you know exactly what works. By customer 10, you're repeating a playbook.

**Deliverable:** A case study or one-pager with your first customer's results. Use it as your selling tool.

## What This Is NOT

- This is not a content marketing strategy. You're not blogging or posting on social yet.
- This is not a paid ads strategy. You're not buying Google Ads or Facebook ads.
- This is not a webinar or demo call series. You're talking to one person at a time.
- This is not passive. You're doing the work yourself — calling, demoing, following up.

**That's the point.** You need to talk to enough daycare owners to know if you're solving a real problem. This plan gets you there in 30 days, using your time, not your budget.

---

## One More Thing

**You're going to hear "no" a lot.** Some owners will say they don't need another tool. Some will say they can't afford it. Some will go silent. That's normal.

But when you find the one who lights up — who gets it instantly, who sees the report card and smiles, who asks when they can start paying — *that's* the signal. That's the person you'll use to convince the next nine.

Good luck. You've got this.
