You're not running a campaign — you're having 40 conversations with daycare owners to find 10 who need what you're building. Success looks like:

- **Month 1:** 15 face-to-face meetings, 5 trial signups, 2 paying customers
- **Month 2:** 15 more meetings, 5 more trials, 8 total paying customers
- **By day 60:** 10 customers paying $X/month, each with a 48-hour onboarding story you can use as social proof

This plan is built around your geographic advantage and your willingness to do direct, high-touch sales. Every tactic here is something *you* can execute alone in a week.

## The Strategy: Why This Approach Works for You Right Now

### 1. **Hyperlocal, High-Touch > Spray-and-Pray**

You live in Irving. Target daycares live within 15 miles of you. That's not a weakness — that's your unfair advantage over every SaaS company emailing from San Francisco.

Your first 10 customers aren't going to come from a landing page or Facebook ads. They're going to come from:
- You walking into a daycare and talking to the owner
- You being referred by another daycare owner who's already using TailWag
- You becoming visible in the local daycare ecosystem (Facebook groups, meetups, word-of-mouth)

**Principle:** Direct sales in 2026 mean using your real relationships and physical presence more, not less.

### 2. **Lead with Insight, Not Pitch**

Every daycare owner you talk to has the same problem: they want more Google reviews and happier clients, but they're drowning in the day-to-day. They get pitched by software vendors all the time. The pitch is invisible to them.

Instead, lead with something valuable:
- A one-page audit of their Google review strategy vs. their top 3 local competitors
- A quick analysis of how many "silent leavers" they might have (based on their review count vs. estimated client base)
- A simple ROI calculator: "If you prevented just 2 no-shows a month, you'd save $X"

This does two things: (1) it proves you understand their world, and (2) it puts TailWag in context as a *solution* to a real problem you just identified.

### 3. **Demo in Their World, Not Yours**

When you do show them TailWag, don't just show them the interface. Show them what it would look like *in their daycare*. Use their name, reference a dog you saw on their Instagram, show what a personalized report card would look like for *their* clients.

The goal: they should think "wait, this already feels like mine."

### 4. **Build Momentum Through Proof**

Your first customer is hard. Your second is easier because you have a story. By the time you're closing customer 8, you'll have:
- A real testimonial from a daycare owner they might know or respect
- Before/after numbers (Google reviews gained, no-shows prevented, etc.)
- A case study you can leave behind

Each customer makes the next one easier. This is why it's critical to *close* that first customer quickly and make their onboarding spectacular.

## The Action Plan: What to Do This Week (and Beyond)

### **PHASE 1: BUILD YOUR TARGET LIST (Days 1–5)**

**Goal:** Create a list of 40 target daycares in the DFW area ranked by fit.

#### 1.1: Google Map Search for Prospects

*How to do it:*

1. Open Google Maps and search: "dog daycare near Irving, TX"
2. Set radius to 15 miles (you want to be able to visit in person)
3. Screenshot or export the list (click each one, note: business name, address, phone, Google rating, review count, website/social)

*Why it matters:* Google gives you your initial pool. You're looking for daycares with:
- 3–4.5 stars (not perfect — this suggests they have the right instinct but room to grow)
- 10–50 reviews (small enough to be independent, established enough to have an audience)
- 2–15 employees (big enough to be busy, small enough that the owner is still hands-on)

*What success looks like:* 30–50 daycares, organized in a spreadsheet

---

#### 1.2: Audit Their Online Presence

*How to do it:*

For each daycare on your list, spend 2 minutes on:
- Their Google Business Profile (rating, review count, when reviews last came in)
- Their Instagram or Facebook (how often they post, engagement rate, what they're showing)
- Their website or booking platform (does it exist? is it from 2015?)

*Why it matters:* This tells you their pain. A daycare with amazing Instagram but only 12 Google reviews is a perfect fit — they're good at creating content but missing the channel that actually converts. A daycare with no reviews and no social presence is struggling on multiple fronts.

*What success looks like:* For each daycare, a 1–2 sentence note: "Strong Instagram, weak Google presence. Good fit." This becomes your targeting rubric.

---

#### 1.3: Rank Your List

*How to do it:*

Score each daycare 1–10 on fit:
- **Google review gap** (have they won the review game or is it a big opportunity?) — 4 points
- **Social media presence** (are they already thinking about marketing?) — 3 points
- **Team size** (are they likely to feel the pain of manual texts?) — 2 points
- **Geographic proximity** (can you visit this week?) — 1 point

Your top 20 are targets for phase 1. These are the daycares most likely to say yes because they already feel the pain.

*What success looks like:* A ranked spreadsheet of your top 20 targets. Top 3 are your "dream closes" for this week.

---

### **PHASE 2: RESEARCH & PERSONALIZE (Days 3–7)**

**Goal:** Know each of your top 10 targets well enough to reference something specific when you reach out.

#### 2.1: Dig Into Their Story

*How to do it:*

For your top 10 targets, spend 15 minutes each:
- Read their Google reviews (what do clients praise? what are common complaints?)
- Scroll their Instagram/Facebook to the end (when did they start? what does their content look like over time?)
- Look for their owner's name and any personal details (where are they from? how long have they been in business?)

*Why it matters:* This is how you personalize. Instead of a generic cold email, you can write: "I saw your review from Sarah Johnson last month about how her golden retriever has a 'new best friend' at your place — that's the kind of client experience that deserves five reviews, not one."

*What success looks like:* A 3–4 sentence snapshot for each target daycare. The first sentence references something specific you found.

---

#### 2.2: Create Your "Insight Lead" For Each Target

*How to do it:*

For your top 5, draft a simple one-page "audit" or "benchmark" you'll give them. It should:
- Compare their Google review metrics (rating, count, recent activity) to 2–3 nearby competitors
- Estimate their "review rate" (reviews per month) and project where they could be
- Include one concrete insight: "You're getting 4x fewer Google reviews than [Competitor], despite similar online presence. Here's one lever that could change that."

*Format:* Simple, professional, TailWag-branded. Looks like you spent 20 minutes on it (not an hour), but it's clearly thoughtful.

You're not selling here — you're helping them see their own problem clearly.

*What success looks like:* 5 one-pagers ready to hand to people or email. Each is personalized to that specific daycare.

### **PHASE 3: FIRST CONTACT (Days 8–14)**

**Goal:** Get 15 conversations scheduled. You're aiming for a 30% conversion rate on outreach (5 meetings per 15 tries).

#### 3.1: Choose Your Outreach Channel

*What works best for daycare owners:*

**In-person visits (preferred, 60% conversion):**
- Show up in person during slow hours (11:30am–2pm)
- Bring your one-pager and be genuinely curious about their business
- "Hi, I'm Summer. I noticed your daycare online and wanted to meet you — I'm helping daycare owners with something that might help your review strategy. Do you have 10 minutes?"
- If they don't have time: "No problem at all. Could I leave this with you and swing by next Tuesday?" (See section 3.3 on the handoff.)

**Phone calls (secondary, 35% conversion):**
- Call, don't email first. Call is faster and gives them less time to put you on the backlog.
- Script: "Hi [name], this is Summer with TailWag. I'm not trying to sell you anything — I've been looking at how independent daycares are doing on Google reviews, and I put together a quick one-pager for [your daycare name] that I thought you'd find interesting. Can I send it your way or would it be easier if I swung by?"
- If they say yes to the one-pager: "Great. I'll email it now. Could I follow up Thursday?" (See section 3.3 on follow-up.)

**Facebook/email (tertiary, 20% conversion):**
- Message them on Facebook or email if you can find their business email. Keep it short and value-first.
- Example: "Hi [name] — I put together a quick breakdown of how [your daycare] is doing on Google reviews vs. nearby competitors. Thought you might find it interesting. It's one page. https://[link to your one-pager]"
- Tone: helpful stranger, not sales pitch.

*Why this order:* In-person is harder for you but works best. Phone is middle ground. Email/Facebook is easiest but has the lowest conversion. Do as much in-person as you can this week (aim for 3–5 visits), phone for the rest.

*What success looks like:* 15 attempts, 5–8 conversations booked

---

#### 3.2: The One-Pager Handoff

*How to do it:*

If you visit in person and they don't have 10 minutes:
1. "No worries at all. I put this together for you — [hand over the one-pager] — it's just a quick look at your review strategy. Could I swing back by Thursday at 12:30pm to get your thoughts?"
2. Or: "Do you have 30 seconds? I'll read it to you." (Read the three most important insights aloud in under a minute.)
3. Get their phone number. Text them the same one-pager that afternoon: "Hey [name] — great meeting you. Here's that one-pager I mentioned. Looking forward to chatting Thursday."

*Why it matters:* You're making a specific commitment to come back. That commitment turns you from a random visitor into someone they're expecting. Much higher close rate.

---

#### 3.3: The Follow-Up Sequence

*Timeline for each contact:*

**Day 0 (initial contact):** In-person meeting or phone call. If no meeting, they get your one-pager.

**Day 1 (next day):** Text or email them. "Great talking with you yesterday. I'm really interested in how you're thinking about reviews. Could we grab 15 minutes this week?" Include your calendar link (Calendly) so they can just book.

**Day 3:** If they haven't booked, send one piece of value: a link to a recent article about how Google reviews impact booking rates, or a stat: "Did you know 72% of people check Google reviews before booking? I pulled your competitor's data — they're getting a new review every 3 days. Curious what your pace looks like."

**Day 7:** Gentle close. "I put together something I think could help your Google strategy. Could we jump on a quick call Friday?" If they say no, move them to your "maybe later" list and reach out again in 30 days.

*What success looks like:* 5 meetings booked for week 2. You're talking to 5 daycare owners who are curious about what you're building.

### **PHASE 4: DEMO & TRIAL (Days 15–35)**

**Goal:** 5 trial signups. You're aiming to convert 50% of your conversations into trials.

#### 4.1: The Meeting Agenda (15 minutes)

*Why you're here:* Not to sell. To understand their world and show them what TailWag could do for *them*.

*What you do:*

1. **Ask before you show (first 5 min)**
   - "Walk me through your week. How many texts are you sending to clients? How long does it take?"
   - "When was the last time you got a Google review? Do you ask for them?"
   - "What's the biggest pain point in your day right now?"

   *Goal:* You want to hear them say something like "I wish I had time to text clients after every drop-off" or "We never get reviews." This is your landing pad for the demo.

2. **Show their world (next 8 min)**
   - Pull up TailWag. Use *their daycare name*. Show what a personalized report card would look like for *their clients*.
   - Reference something specific: "I saw on your Instagram you posted about Bella the golden retriever last week. Imagine the mom gets a text at 5pm that says 'Bella had an amazing day — she made three new friends at group play and nailed her manners training. See you tomorrow!' That's what this does."
   - Show the review request. Show the appointment reminder.
   - **Don't oversell.** Keep it simple. One daycare owner said recently: "I just want to know my dog is happy and when to pick them up."

3. **Remove objections (final 2 min)**
   - "It's literally five minutes to set up. I can walk you through it."
   - "You only start paying when you're happy with the first week. And it pays for itself in one prevented no-show."
   - **Ask for the trial.** "Would it make sense to try this for a week? You'd see what real report cards look like for your clients."

*What success looks like:* They say yes to a trial. You get their permission to set up an account and send their first report card Friday morning.

---

#### 4.2: The Trial Setup

*How to do it:*

1. **Same day** (while you're still there or that evening): Set up their account, pull in their business details, and preview the first message
2. **Next morning:** Send their first report card to a test client. Frame it: "Your first report card just went out. Can you show me the client's reaction?"
3. **Day 3:** Check in. "How are people responding?" Collect any feedback.
4. **Day 7:** Close conversation. "What would it take for you to become a customer?"

*What success looks like:* Trial going live Friday, feedback by Monday, close conversation by Friday of week 2.

---

#### 4.3: The Close

*By day 7 of the trial, you know whether they're going to buy or not.* If they are:

- **Offer a trial period with a safety net:** "First month is $X, but if you don't see results after 30 days, I'll refund you."
- **Or offer a "pay for what you get" model:** "We keep the lights on for a week. Once you see your first report cards going out and getting responses, you pay $X/month."
- **Make onboarding irresistible:** "I'm going to stay on the phone with you for 20 minutes on day 1 to make sure everything works. You won't be figuring this out alone."

*What success looks like:* Customer 1 pays. Now you have a story.

### **PHASE 5: BUILD MOMENTUM (Days 35–60)**

**Goal:** 8 total paying customers by day 60.

#### 5.1: Turn Your First Customer Into Social Proof

As soon as customer 1 is live (day 3–5):

1. **Get a testimonial:** "Can I grab a quick voice memo of you walking me through what changed in your first week?" (Video or voice memo is more powerful than written.)

You're going to use this relentlessly in the next phase.

#### 5.2: Referral Loop

As soon as you have a paying customer:

1. **Ask for introductions:** "Do you know any other daycare owners in the DFW area who struggle with reviews or no-shows? Could I mention that you're using TailWag?"
2. **Offer a referral bonus:** "If you refer someone who becomes a customer, I'll give you a free month or a month at 50% off." (You can afford this.)
3. **Make the intro easy:** "I'll send you an email you can forward, or I can set up a call with you both."

Referrals from a happy customer convert at 60–70%. They're gold.

---

#### 5.3: Tighten Your Funnel

By day 35, you've run 15 conversations. You know what works and what doesn't. Analyze:

- Which daycares converted? What did they have in common?
- Which objections came up most? How did you handle them?
- What part of your pitch landed hardest?

By week 6, you're running a tighter version of week 1. You've eliminated weak targets and sharpened your message.

#### 5.4: Secondary Outreach Channels (Week 5+)

Once you have 2–3 customers, you can add these without diluting your direct sales efforts:

1. **Local Facebook groups** — Post value, not pitches. "Hey, I've been researching Google review strategies for daycares. Happy to share what I found." When people ask, you have customers to reference.

2. **Local chamber of commerce or pet industry meetups** — Attend, meet people, hand out your case study. You're not selling — you're networking with the 5 people who might know a daycare owner.

3. **Dog trainer/groomer referral partnerships** — Visit trainers and groomers nearby. "If your clients ask about daycare recommendations, I know a few great places. Could I leave some info with you?" (Reciprocal: if they get referred as a trainer, you're happy to mention them.)

4. **Nextdoor** — Post in the Nextdoor neighborhood app. Local, trusted audience. A simple post: "I'm helping local dog daycares get more Google reviews. Curious if other dog parents notice daycares without reviews?" Opens conversations.

None of these replace direct sales. But they reinforce it and create serendipitous conversations.

## Messaging: What to Say and How to Say It

### The One-Liner (When You Have 10 Seconds)

"I help dog daycares turn happy clients into Google reviews and cut no-shows. Basically, automated texts that do what you're probably doing manually — but actually get sent."

### The Elevator Pitch (30 Seconds)

"You know how you want to text clients at pickup and tell them how their dog's day went, but you never have time? TailWag does that automatically — personalized to each dog, timed to land in their inbox at exactly the right moment. Plus, it asks for reviews and sends appointment reminders. Takes five minutes to set up."

### The Problem-First Opener (For Cold Meetings)

"I've been looking at Google reviews for independent daycares in the DFW area, and I noticed something: most daycares have amazing experiences but barely any reviews to show for it. Parents pick based on reviews. You're probably losing clients who never even check you out. I put together a one-pager showing where you're at vs. nearby daycares. Thought you'd find it interesting."

### The Demo Close

"Here's what I'd love to do: let me set up an account for [Daycare Name] and send one real report card to a client tomorrow morning. You'll see exactly what your clients are getting. Then we can talk about what happens next. Fair?"

### The Objection Handlers

**"I don't have time for another tool."**
"I get it. But you're already thinking about these clients — you're just doing it in your head. This just gets the thoughts out of your head and to the parents. Saves you time because you're not stressing about it."

**"My clients are already happy."**
"They probably are. But here's the thing: happy clients who don't leave reviews are invisible to new parents. And the ones who drift away? They usually don't say anything — they just book somewhere else. TailWag fixes the silent ones."

**"How much does it cost?"**
"$[X] a month. But here's how we'd do it: try it for a week. You'll see your first report cards landing. Then we talk pricing. If it's not working, you're done. No contract, no hassle. One prevented no-show pays for the whole month."

**"I'll think about it."**
"That's fair. What's the best way for me to follow up? Email? Text? Let me send you the one-pager and we can touch base Thursday. Does that work?"

## Avoiding the Big Mistakes

1. **Trying to scale too early.** Don't build a landing page or Facebook ad campaign before you've closed 5 customers. You don't know your pitch yet. Direct sales first, scaling later.

2. **Not closing fast enough.** The gap between "interested" and "paying" is where most deals die. Get them to a trial in a meeting. Get them paying in a week. Don't let momentum die.

3. **Pitching features instead of outcomes.** They don't care that your AI is "advanced." They care that they'll get more Google reviews and prevent no-shows. Lead with outcomes.

4. **Not following up consistently.** Most sales in B2B happen in the follow-up. You're going to feel like you're bothering people. You're not. You're the third person to ask — the other two were your competitors.

5. **Trying to appeal to every daycare.** You're not. Your first 10 customers are going to be a specific profile: probably owners who already care about their online presence, who are actively trying to grow, who've gotten a bad review and want to fix it. Find them. Ignore the ones who say "no." There are plenty of fish.
