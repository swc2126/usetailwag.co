# Daycare Walk-In Demo Preparation Guide

## The Strategy

A walk-in demo is your strongest weapon right now. You're not sending an email that disappears into a crowded inbox — you're showing up in person, showing respect for their time, and letting them see TailWag work with their actual business. This is the kind of personal touch that independent daycare owners value and remember.

The goal isn't to close a deal in 15 minutes. It's to:
1. **Show them a moment of clarity** — what it would feel like if they didn't have to manually text 40 pet parents every day
2. **Plant a seed** — "I see your potential and I'm building something specifically for daycares like yours"
3. **Build permission for follow-up** — Leave them with a reason to talk to you again

The daycare owner is busy, probably skeptical of software pitches, and certainly doesn't have time for a long conversation. So your job is to be fast, friendly, respectful of their time, and show up as a genuine peer who understands their world — not a salesperson.

---

## What to Bring

Bring less than you think you need. Physical items should either demonstrate immediate value or make you memorable. Here's what works:

### Essential
1. **Your phone or tablet with the TailWag demo pre-loaded**
   - Have the app ready to go (no log-in, no loading screens, no fumbling)
   - Pre-populate it with *their daycare's name* and 3–4 dogs from their Instagram (use their real dog names)
   - Have sample report cards pre-written and ready to show
   - Test the WiFi at the daycare before you demo (or use a hotspot)

2. **A one-page leave-behind** (physical paper)
   - Headline: "How [DaycareName] Could Generate 12 More 5-Star Reviews This Month"
   - Three sections: the problem (silent leavers, manual texting), the solution (what TailWag does), the ROI (dollar amount based on their specific review count)
   - Include your phone number, email, and a link to a simple calendar for a follow-up call
   - Make it beautiful — not corporate, but professional. Use TailWag brand colors, clean typography, one high-quality dog photo
   - Print it on cardstock, not regular paper

3. **A very short script** (written on a card for you to reference)
   - Bullet points only, not full sentences
   - For moments where your mind goes blank under pressure
   - See "The Demo Script" section below

### Optional but Nice
- A small treat for the dogs (if the owner seems open to it) — shows you're a real dog person
- A handwritten note on the back of your one-pager ("Summer from TailWag — great meeting you! Here's that report card example I showed you")
- A voice memo link or Loom video you can text them after the visit (5–10 seconds showing their daycare name + a sample report card)

### What NOT to Bring
- Printed decks or long documents
- Multiple devices
- Anything that requires them to sign or commit on the spot
- Business cards with a lengthy service description

## The Demo Script (What to Say)

### First 30 Seconds: The Opener
You have one job here: get permission to show them something and prove you're not a typical software salesperson.

**What you say:**
"Hey [Owner Name], thanks so much for a minute — I know you're busy. I'm Summer, I built TailWag. I was looking at your Google reviews and something jumped out at me. You've got mostly 5-star reviews, but you're at [X reviews]. I bet you've got at least [2X] clients who had an amazing experience and never left a review. Does that feel about right?"

*Listen. Let them agree or add context.*

"Yeah, most daycares I talk to have that same gap. So I built something that automatically sends personalized report cards to pet parents at pickup time, plus reminder texts that cut no-shows. And here's the thing — it actually works because it's built for daycares, not just a generic texting tool. I have a 30-second demo if you want to see it?"

**Why this works:**
- You led with insight about *their specific business*, not a pitch
- You showed you did homework (looked at their reviews)
- You acknowledged the gap they didn't even know they had (silent leavers)
- You asked permission before launching into a demo
- You set expectations (30 seconds, not 15 minutes)

### The 30-Second Demo
This is where you show magic. The goal is for them to think: "Wait, this is already made for my daycare?"

**What you do:**
1. Pull out your phone and show them the dashboard with their daycare's name at the top
2. Show one sample report card with a dog's name from their Instagram — something like:
   > "Hey Sarah! Daisy had the BEST day at TailWag. She played hard with the crew all morning, crushed it on a long walk, and passed out around 2pm like she owned the place. Can't wait to see her tomorrow! 🐾"
3. Say: "This goes out automatically at pickup time. No typing, no copy-pasting. The AI writes it personalized to what actually happened that day."
4. Swipe to the reminder screen and show: "And this reminder goes out 24 hours before their appointment — cuts no-shows by about 30% on average."
5. Swipe to the review request: "Then, when things are going great, this asks for a review at exactly the right moment."

**Don't say:**
- Feature details ("It uses natural language processing…")
- Price
- Technical jargon
- Anything about setup or integration

**Do say:**
- "Takes 5 minutes to set up"
- "Runs in the background"
- "You're already doing this work — we just automate it"

### The Close (60 Seconds)
You've shown them something valuable. Now make it easy to say yes to a next step.

**What you say:**
"I'm launching TailWag in the next few weeks with early customers here in DFW. I'd love to get you in as a beta user — which means you get it free for the first month, and I get your feedback on what actually matters to daycare owners. No commitment. What would work better — a 15-minute call this week to talk through how it would work, or do you want to try it live for a day?"

**Why this works:**
- You're offering value (free month)
- You're asking for permission, not pushing
- You gave two options (both move forward)
- You're specific about next steps (15-min call, or trial day)
- You're not trying to close the deal in the room

## How to Handle Objections

Daycare owners will raise these. Here's how to handle each one with grace and without sounding defensive.

### Objection 1: "My clients are already happy."

**What you hear:** They don't see the problem because they can't see the clients who quietly left.

**What you say:**
"I hear that, and honestly, it usually means you're doing a great job. But here's what I found when I talked to [other local daycare owner's name] — she felt the same way. Then we looked at her Google reviews. She had 150+ clients in the past three years, but only 20 reviews. That meant 130 happy people who never took 30 seconds to leave a review. One review averages 3 more clients a month for daycares like yours. So there's probably $5k–$10k of revenue sitting on the table — not because your clients aren't happy, but because asking is hard to remember."

**Why this works:**
- You validate their instinct (they ARE doing well)
- You show data, not opinion
- You reference a peer, not just theory
- You translate the problem into dollars

### Objection 2: "I don't need another software tool."

**What you hear:** They're already overwhelmed by systems. They don't want to learn something new or add another login.

**What you say:**
"Totally fair — you've probably already got a booking system, maybe a POS, maybe a CRM. I'm not asking you to learn something new or add another password to remember. TailWag *replaces* work you're already doing. Right now, you're either texting clients manually or you're *not* doing it because there's no time. TailWag runs in the background — you literally don't think about it. Does that make sense?"

**Why this works:**
- You acknowledge their real pain (tool fatigue)
- You reframe it as replacing work, not adding work
- You emphasize simplicity
- You ask a soft closing question

### Objection 3: "I can just text them myself."

**What you hear:** They don't think they need help. (They're wrong, but they don't know it yet.)

**What you say:**
"You absolutely can. Most daycare owners tell me that too — and then I ask them this: 'Last week, how many of your 40 clients got a personalized text about their dog's day?' [Wait for answer — it's usually 'a few' or 'none.'] That's the reality, right? Not because you don't want to, but because by the end of the day, you're tired, you've got cleanup, the next client is arriving. The intent is there, but time isn't. TailWag is basically you, but you don't get tired. It sends every single day, personalized, so your clients feel seen."

**Why this works:**
- You don't argue — you ask them a question that proves the point
- You show empathy for the real constraint (time, not will)
- You reframe TailWag as an extension of them, not a replacement
- You tie it back to client relationship value

### Objection 4: "How much does it cost?"

**What you say (at this stage):**
"Great question — I'm still figuring out exact pricing, but I'm thinking $99–$199 a month depending on the number of clients. For context, if you get even *one* no-show prevented per month, or one extra Google review, it pays for itself. For you, with [their client count], it's almost certainly covering itself in recovered revenue in the first few weeks. I'll send you pricing as soon it's locked. Does that ballpark feel reasonable?"

**Why this works:**
- You're honest (still figuring it out — they'll respect that as a founder)
- You give a range, not a hard number (you have flexibility)
- You lead with ROI, not cost
- You make a soft assumption (it'll pay for itself) that they won't argue with

**If they push back:** "I know it's an investment. Here's what I'm thinking for early customers like you — free first month, so you can see the actual impact before you commit to anything. Fair?"

### Objection 5: "I'd have to ask [partner/manager/etc.]"

**What you say:**
"Of course. Here's what I'll do — I'll send you a quick one-pager with the basics and maybe a 2-minute video showing how it works. You can show them that, and if they have questions, just have them reach out to me directly. Sound good? What email should I send it to?"

**Why this works:**
- You make it easy for them to advocate for you
- You offer to take the next step (no passive "let me know")
- You're ready to answer questions from the decision-maker
- You get their email in the process

## Before You Walk In: The Checklist

- [ ] Research their Google reviews, Instagram, Facebook — know their vibe and their dog roster
- [ ] Pre-load your demo with their daycare name and 3–4 dogs from their photos (use real dog names)
- [ ] Write 2–3 sample report cards for their specific dogs and save them as screenshots
- [ ] Test the demo on your phone/tablet — no lag, no loading errors
- [ ] Print 3 copies of your one-pager (one for them, one backup, one for you to reference)
- [ ] Have your script card in your pocket
- [ ] Plan your opening — how will you get 30 seconds of their time? (First thing in the morning? Quiet moment mid-day? After pickup rush?)
- [ ] Charge your phone and have a backup charger
- [ ] Set a calendar reminder the next morning to send your follow-up

## Right After the Demo: The Follow-Up (Same Day or Next Morning)

Send them:
1. **A text message** (get their number during the visit, or ask for it at the end):
   > "Hey [Owner Name], thanks again for a few minutes this morning! Here's that one-pager I mentioned. Excited to show you what TailWag can do for [DaycareName]. Let me know if you have questions. — Summer"
   > [Attach the one-pager or a link]

2. **An email with:**
   - Subject: "Quick follow-up: TailWag demo for [DaycareName]"
   - One sentence: "Thanks again for your time. Here's a custom report card example for your daycare — this is what you'd send to parents automatically each day."
   - Screenshot or PDF of the sample report card
   - A 2–3 sentence close: "I'm bringing TailWag to DFW daycares in April. I'd love to get you in as a beta customer — free first month. When's good for a quick 15-min call to talk through how it works?"
   - Calendar link (Calendly or similar) with 2–3 time slots for the next 3 days

3. **A custom Loom or voice memo** (optional, but memorable):
   - 30–60 seconds showing their daycare name + the sample report card you demoed
   - "Hey [Owner Name], just wanted to drop this here so you could see again how the report card would look for [DaycareName]. Looking forward to talking more. — Summer"
   - Send as a text or embed in the email

## What Success Looks Like

After your walk-in demo, you've succeeded if:

✅ You get 15 minutes of their focused attention (even if it feels shorter)
✅ They see a report card with their daycare name and actual dogs from their life
✅ They say something like "Oh, I see — that's something my clients would love" or "Yeah, people are always asking how their dogs did"
✅ They take your one-pager and don't immediately throw it away
✅ They give you their phone number or email without you having to ask twice
✅ They say yes (or "maybe") to a follow-up call
✅ They mention you to someone else they know (you'll hear this in a future conversation)

You've *over-succeeded* if:
- They ask about pricing unprompted
- They ask when they can start using it
- They mention specific pain points (no-shows, quiet leavers, manual texting) that TailWag solves
- They ask you to come back and demo to their team

## A Few Mindset Reminders

**You're not selling software. You're solving a specific problem for a specific person.**
The daycare owner doesn't care about your tech stack or your vision — they care about whether you understand their day and whether TailWag makes it easier. Lead with empathy and data, not features.

**This person doesn't know you yet.**
First impressions matter. Show up on time, be friendly, remember their name, ask about their dogs. You're building a relationship, not just trying to land a customer. The sale happens in the follow-up anyway.

**You have permission to take up space.**
You built something for daycares. You're local. You care enough to show up in person. That's rare and valuable. Don't apologize for pitching — just do it respectfully and make it worth their time.

**If they're not interested, it's not about you.**
Some daycares aren't ready. Some have a different tech setup. Some are too small. That's fine. Leave them better off than when you arrived (they got a useful one-pager, you were friendly, no hard feelings), and move on to the next one. Your job is to find the daycares who are hungry for this, not to convince everyone.

---

## Next Steps After the Call

If they say yes to a follow-up call:
1. Send a calendar invite the same day with a clear agenda: "Let's spend 15 minutes talking through how TailWag would work for your specific setup — no pressure, just exploring if it's a fit."
2. Before the call, look up any recent reviews or social media posts — reference something specific
3. On the call, focus on their needs, not your product. Listen more than you talk.
4. Offer a trial: "How about we set you up for free for a week? You'll see actual report cards going to your clients, you can test it, and then we'll jump on a quick call to see if it makes sense."

You've got this. Go show them what you built.
