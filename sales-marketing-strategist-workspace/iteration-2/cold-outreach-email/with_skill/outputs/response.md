# Cold Outreach Email for Daycare Owners

## The Strategy

This email is built on three principles that make it different from generic cold pitches:

1. **Lead with insight, not product.** The opener references something specific you've noticed (their Google rating or review count), which shows you've done homework and gives you credibility before you ask for anything.

2. **Name the pain first.** The email acknowledges the exact problem daycare owners face — silent churn, the admin burden of texting clients, the hard truth that only 2% of happy clients leave reviews. This builds immediate rapport.

3. **Show value upfront.** You're offering a free one-pager analyzing their online presence compared to nearby competitors. This is *useful* — not a pitch disguised as helpfulness. It demonstrates expertise and gives them a reason to reply other than "I want to hear about your product."

4. **Personal touch, not template.** Use their daycare's name, reference a specific detail from their Google profile or social media, ask a real question. Make it impossible to miss that this email was written for them.

The tone is warm and confident — you're not asking for permission to help, you're offering it.

---

## The Email (Ready to Send)

**Subject line (choose one):**
- `Quick question about [Daycare Name]'s Google presence`
- `[Daycare Name] — your Google rating opportunity`
- `Why 98% of your happy clients don't leave reviews`

**From:** Summer (or "Summer from TailWag" if you want to hint at the product)

**Email body:**

---

Hi [Daycare Owner Name],

I was looking at [Daycare Name]'s Google profile today — 4.2 stars with 23 reviews. I know you because [specific detail: "your Instagram shows some seriously happy pups" or "I drove past your location on [street] and saw the play yard setup" or "I saw you're booked solid on weekends"].

Here's what caught my attention: your Google rating is good, but only 3 clients have left reviews in the past year. That's a silent churn problem most daycare owners don't see coming until it's too late.

The hard truth — 98% of happy clients never leave a review. They just quietly stop coming. Meanwhile, the 2% of unhappy ones absolutely will leave one.

I work with independent daycares on exactly this. And I put together a quick one-pager analyzing [Daycare Name]'s online presence versus 3 other daycares in the [specific neighborhood/area]. It shows:

- Your actual review velocity (how many you're getting per month)
- How you stack up against nearby competitors
- The revenue impact of a 0.5-star rating difference

No strings attached — I'll email it over if you're curious. Or if this isn't a priority right now, totally understand.

Either way, the dogs sound like they're having a great time. That's the hard part.

---

Summer
TailWag
[your phone number]
summer@usetailwag.com

## How to Use This

### Before you send:

1. **Do the research.** Find 3–5 specific daycares on Google in your target area. Pull their review count, rating, and when the last reviews came in. Grab a detail from their social media — a specific dog name, a mention of a service they offer, anything that shows you looked.

2. **Fill in the blanks.** Replace [Daycare Name] and [Owner Name]. Make the specific detail real — not generic.

3. **Create the one-pager.** This is the hook. Use a Google Sheet or a simple PDF showing:
   - Their current Google rating + review count
   - Their "review velocity" (e.g., "averaging 1 new review every 45 days")
   - A comparison to 2–3 nearby daycares (anonymized is fine, or name them if they're known competitors)
   - The ROI of a 0.5-star rating bump (you can estimate this: "a 4.7 rating vs. 4.2 typically results in 15–20% more new client inquiries")

   Make it one page. Make it look clean and professional. The design should match TailWag's aesthetic if possible (green accents, warm cream background, clear numbers in a larger font).

4. **Pick your outreach channel.** Email is fine, but consider these alternatives:
   - **Text message** if you can find their cell (public phone number on Google Business Profile): "Hi [name], quick question — do you track how many of your happy clients leave Google reviews? I was looking at [daycare name]'s profile and noticed something interesting. Open to a 2-min call?"
   - **LinkedIn** if they have a profile (less common for daycare owners, but try)
   - **Instagram DM** — many daycare owners are active here. "Hey! Love [Daycare Name]'s feed — those pups look happy. Quick thought about your Google reviews — want me to send a free analysis?"
   - **In-person visit** — this is the nuclear option and most effective. Show up during a slow time (late afternoon), bring the one-pager printed on nice paper, introduce yourself, leave it behind, ask if you can follow up.

### After you send:

- **Expect a 5–15% response rate.** That's strong for cold email. If you're not getting responses, the daycare name/detail isn't specific enough, or the one-pager isn't compelling.
- **First follow-up (2 days later):** "Did the one-pager get to you?" Keep it short.
- **Second follow-up (1 week later):** Share a relevant stat or article about Google reviews and daycare business. "Saw this and thought of our conversation — most of your happy clients probably didn't know you'd love a review."
- **Third follow-up (2 weeks later):** "I'll stop reaching out, but would love to grab 15 minutes sometime. No pitch, just want to understand what's working for you guys and what's not."

### What success looks like:

- You get a reply saying "yeah, that's interesting — tell me more"
- You get a call request
- You get an in-person meeting to show the demo

Any of those means you're in the sales conversation. From there, the goal is a 15-minute exploratory call where you ask questions more than you pitch.

## Why This Works (The Psychology)

1. **Specificity signals respect.** Generic outreach says "I'm mass-emailing everyone." Specific outreach says "I actually care about your business."

2. **You're naming a real problem they have but haven't quantified.** Most daycare owners feel the silent churn but don't have numbers around it. Giving them a metric ("98% of happy clients don't leave reviews") validates their intuition and creates urgency.

3. **The free analysis is a "reciprocity hook."** You're giving value before asking for anything. They feel indebted to at least look at what you sent.

4. **You're positioning yourself as a peer, not a vendor.** The tone is friendly and genuine, not salesy. You're not trying to close them in one email — you're opening a conversation.

5. **The personal detail prevents this from being spam.** They know you looked at their business specifically. It's harder for them to ignore.

## Variation: If You Want to Be Bolder

If you want to lead with product insight instead of review analysis, here's an alternative opening:

---

Hi [Name],

I noticed [Daycare Name] texted your clients a few times last month (saw a client mention it on Facebook). That's awesome — most daycares don't do that at all.

Here's what I'm curious about: how long does it usually take you to write those texts? And how often do you actually send them?

Because almost every daycare owner I talk to says "I know I should be texting clients more, but I just don't have the time." They end up sending maybe 2–3 texts a month when they mean to send one a week.

We built something specifically for this — personalized, AI-generated report cards that go out automatically after each visit. I'm testing it with a few daycares in DFW and want to learn what's actually missing from their current setup.

Would you be open to a 15-minute call to talk through what "perfect" would look like for you?

---

This version works if you've spotted evidence that they *already care* about client communication but are struggling with execution. It's less about their pain and more about their aspiration — which can be equally motivating.

---

## One Last Thing

The most important variable in cold outreach isn't the email — it's your follow-up consistency. Most daycare owners will ignore the first email. They'll respond to the third one, or the fifth one. Don't get discouraged after email #1. You're not trying to close them in one message; you're trying to get in front of them repeatedly until they're ready to talk.

Good luck. 🐾
